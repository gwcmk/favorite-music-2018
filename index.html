My Favorite Music of 2018

Greg Kim December 31, 2018

Albums/Mixtapes/EPs

  1. Earl Sweatshirt - Some Rap Songs

    I was going through some dark times this year, and because of that I deliberately avoided carefully listening to this album. I consider Earl to be one of the greatest rappers alive today due of his technical ability. But what puts him in the conversation of one of the all time greats is his songwriting ability. Some of his raps from earlier years are the most disturbingly lucid depictions of depression and anxiety that I've heard. Given that he went through his father's death earlier this year, I was hesitant to put myself in that headspace.

    I'm in a bit of a better place now, so I've spent the past week really listening to this album. The more I listen to it and unpack the stuff he's saying...I don't think I've ever heard anything this heavy.

    From his great interview with Vulture, Earl discussed the imperfections and subtleties of Some Rap Songs:

    "Yeah, it’s more human. ... I always just revert back to when I was younger because that’s when you haven’t learned so much, and all this bullshit hasn’t become, like, calloused on your brain. I go off what would make me soar in my room by myself as a child."

    It's often easy to be thinking too much when listening to music, especially if that's what is expected by the artist. When I hear someone going off, rapping as fast as they can, I'm no longer interested in keeping up. There's a bit of authenticity lost there. How can you really be aiming to evoke any kind of genuine response? No one thinks like that. What's much more interesting is actually expressing yourself and getting to something real, like Earl said.

    And that's what cut through everything for me. The raw feelings as well as the crushing weight of depression were so vivid. I felt the lows of hopelessness, the fleeting but empty reprieve of unhealthy coping, the longing for happiness. As the album closes, the penultimate track "Peanut" is possibly the most emotionally arresting song I've ever heard.

    Flushin' through the pain, depression, this is not a phase, ayy
    Picking out his grave, couldn't help but feel out of place
    Try and catch some rays
    Death, it has the sour taste (Sour taste)
    Bless my pops, we sent him off and not an hour late
    Still in shock and now my heart out somewhere on the range
    Outta range, picked the lock and now we elevate
    We box 'em out, my shit a million miles away

    I haven't experienced anything as traumatic as his father's death, but I couldn't help but be pulled back into it, feeling like you're spiraling with no end in sight and wanting nothing more than to escape. It's overwhelming.

    As it segues into the last track, the instrumental "Riot!", the guitars and horns of the Hugh Masekela sample finally give way to a reprieve. The sounds wobble and fade out, and it's unclear what's next. In place of the absence of hope is a sense of uncertainty but possibly also resolve. In the face of darkness, the best we can do is move forward. I think we all know that, but coming from your lowest point and actually grasping that ideal can be such a painful journey.

  7. Meek Mill - Championships

    This album felt nostalgic to me. Championships is like the pre-Soundcloud big budget blockbuster albums that we used to get from rappers at the top of the game. Pretty much every big name in rap shows up on a feature or production credit. What makes it great though is Meek Mill, back with the same intensity that he's always brought to his albums. Every time you heard him on a track, his verses demanded your attention, and newly reborn with a purpose of criminal justice reform, we now have a really good reason to listen.
